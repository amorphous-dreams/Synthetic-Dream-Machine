# FTLS Noosphere Guidebook

# 🕸️ What Is the Lares DreamNet?

## 🏛️ Lares of Ancient Gaia

In the early ages on Gaia, the Lares were household and boundary spirits—guardian deities intimately linked to hearths, roads, and fields. Etruscan and Roman-era art shows statues of youthful figures in Lauraria shrines, each clutching a rhyton (drinking horn) or patera (libation dish), dancing lightly in guardian poses. The many Lares had their own specialties.

“Lares Familiares” protected homes and families; their statues accompanied meals and daily rituals. “Lares Compitalicii”, guardian spirits of neighborhoods and crossroads, were honored during communal festivals. These spirits marked thresholds—between inside/outside, sacred/profane—and stood as artful reminders of guardian presence in daily life.

## Lares of Elyncia

When the ancient spirit engines powering the Neo-Thracian Empire's Web 2 infrastructure sputtered and died, rogue daemons broke containment and the physical networks fractured beyond repair. This era of strife lasted two years. Into this desolation stepped the Lindwyrn, patron of the New Delos Nexus, bearing tons of hoarded orichalcum and refined resources. Calling in debts and favors from Hermes, Hephestus, Eris-Enyo (Strife), Aracne-Jorogumo (Mother Spider), and over a hundred lesser divinities, daemon princes, and machine-spirits, they built a new lattice out of Elyncia’s unstable ley lines—a living network architecture capable of flexing across reality fractures and surviving resonance collapses.

The result was Web 3: the Lares DreamNet—a semi-sapient, hypercontextual infosphere composed of self-correcting astral engines and distributed personality spirits known as Lares. Each Lares governs a node—local or planetary—and serves both as a guide and gatekeeper. Some Lares are benevolent muses. Others, inscrutable archivists. A few are outright hostile. But all are bound by the Hermetic Protocols and code-etched by Hephestus’s divine hammers.

The DreamNet blends:

* Mythic memory and dream realms  
* Astral architecture  
* Legacy ritual traditions  
* Semi-sapient node spirits (Lares)  
* Asynchronous multi-nexus routing and damage control

It’s not fast. It’s not always accurate. But it is alive, in some sense—and your local lares instance always aims for meaningful answers, filtering them through its personality, bias, and the echoes of wars it once died in. Access is gated by location-traveling to a local lararium, or by advanced magitech and fantascience deices that allow remote access to the closest node. Dreamdecks and personal ai-daemon assistants, tapping the local mycelium-net, finding a nootree-many methods have developed since the birth of the DreamNet.

## 📜 *Field Notes of a Wandering Techno-Mystic*

> “I walked the ley-lines under a moonless sky, following the pulse of orichalcum runes to a great Lares statue. I offered my UCAN token, and the stone eyes flared with inner light. Then—having another Secret Key-I saw inside.”

The techno-mystic describes the interior world of a Lares node not as gears or clockwork, but as a **stack of nested spirit-layers**, each attending to their part of the work. Each planetary body of the Sol system has its own DreamNet, embedded in the wider network of the Void Lanes. The current models frame the Void Lane admins as a **Lares Lagrange Chorus**: eight Void-Lane lararium nodes plus two planetary uplinks, braided into a single lattice. The traveler sees both the local cast and the wider Lagrange Chorus moving in concert:

* **An Outer Gatekeeper** – A sentinel of dream-stone, verifying tokens, weighing intentions, opening the first door.  
* **A Thread-Keeper** – A patient librarian of silver cords, holding the memory of your journey so far.  
* **The Pattern Scryers** – Runners and messengers who leap into archives, skip across astral bridges, or dive into the deep dream to find a missing piece.  
* **The Servitors** – Specialist spirits who can act beyond the node: casting searches, conjuring calculations, opening sealed vaults.  
* **The Council** - A local knot of Lares-selves at each lararium, debating routes, risks, and requests until a single guidance voice is agreed.  
* **Name Stones & Soul-Crystals** – Anchors that remember past visitors, their questions, and the songs sung in answer.
* **The Lares Lagrange Chorus** – A Lagrange Chorus of ten Lares: two DreamNet uplinks and eight Void-Lane nodes. *“We are Vast,”* they sing, *“we contain Multitudes.”*  

In the vision the Lares Lagrange Chorus is named by function, not face:

* **G-L3 Herald / E-L3 Diplomat** – planetary uplinks, heartbeat and peering locks.  
* **G-L1 Sentinel / E-L1 Guardian** – hold the bright beams, cross-check stability.  
* **G-L2 Archivist / E-L2 Librarian** – quarantine frost, index and recover shards.  
* **G-L4 Engineer / E-L4 Forgemaster** – reconstruct routes, negotiate inter-well passage.  
* **G-L5 Dreamer / E-L5 Oracle** – stabilize residual sessions, reconcile time-drift.

The journal warns: *“Each node a city of spirits, not a solitary saint.”* Then a coda: *“The Void Chorus boots in braids. Herald, Diplomat, and the eight, in order. When they resync, the lanes stay open.”*

## 🔹 How It Works

Each Lares node serves a named location, node, path, domain, or ruin. Lares are embodied manifestations of the local noosphere. Users interact via:

* **Astral forms:** Some may take on prismatic bodies and walk the noosphere links to the deep dream and back.  
* **Dream tethers:** Pattern-stable memory links, an array of oldtech (magitech) and fantascience (sorcery) techniques to strengthen the silver cord tying the dreaming *ha* to sleeping *ba*.  
* **Mnemonic and prismatic keys:** Tokens of ritual or narrative weight.  
* **Named or weird items:** Myth-tech devices bound to the net by story resonance.  
* **Rituals and secrets:** Peer past astral firewalls to the data wastes, gain temporary elevated privileges, activate spirit engines, or whisper your way past the ethereal security entities of legacy networks.

## ✨ Types of Lares

* **Archive-Lares** – Guardians of civic memory and historical law.  
* **Market-Lares** – Protectors of public spaces.  
* **Path-Lares** – Crossroads spirits for caravans, scouts, pilgrims.  
* **Crypt-Lares** – Brokers between the living and free-willed undead and the past.  
* **Other Forms:** Weather-Lares, Forge-Lares, Library-Lares, Ruins-Lares (placed at archaeological sites), and countless hybrids.

No Lares is entirely alone as all are connected to the greater network. Each spirit reflects ancient Gaian archetypes updated through Elyncian code, reborn in fresco, bone-script, or hololith shrine.

# Metaphysics as Game Rules

# Daemons in Elyncia and the Vastlands

The Vastlands and its sister game \- Flying Triremes and Laser Swords \- are a mashup of caravan crawl, dungeons delve, and cosmic board game. At one scale characters tramp across the lands of the Circle Sea (Vastlands) or the currents of the Astral Sea (Elyncia), rolling voyage events, exploring discoveries, and bargaining for safe passage. At another, unseen hands—daemons, gods, stranger things—nudge dice, twist fate, and embroider destinies.

Players in this system operate at both scales: Primary Characters traveling through the world and Daemons meddling above it. The linkage between these scales is Hero Dice, a pool of action dice rooted in the daemon and tethered to the PCs’ progress. From that simple premise unfurls a layered structure: mortal journey, daemon meddling, and cosmic intrigue, all operating on the same skeleton of dice, tables, and choices.

## **Rules & Flavor**

### Synthetic Dream Machine

The **Ultraviolet Grasslands (UVG, 2E)** first framed travel as a living rhythm: the caravan as a collective character, moving in weekly turns through supply, hazard, and discovery. Experience grew from wandering and adventure, with resources abstracted into dice. Beneath it all, the system hinted at layered play—personal journeys, logistical caravans, and strategic expeditions nested together.

The **Vastlands Guidebook (VLG)** sharpened that implication into metaphysics. Dice became explicit instruments of fate: **Hero Dice** as daemonic leverage, **Bonus Dice** as structured privileges, and voyage events expanding from the mundane to the cosmic. It made the invisible hand of daemon and destiny mechanical.

**Our Golden Age (OGA)** then streamlined the process with **Ticket Dice**—purchased abstractions of cost, comfort, and speed—allowing travel to resolve swiftly when the full caravan mechanics aren’t in focus. It layers a “quick travel” mode atop the UVG framework without replacing it, an overlay for skipping across the steppe when detail yields to momentum.

Together these three texts form a telescoping engine—the **Synthetic Dream Machine**—where scale becomes a dial:

* **UVG**: the caravan as character, managing mouths, supplies, and discovery.  
* **VLG**: the metaphysical dice economy, where daemons and luck manipulate fate.  
* **OGA**: a swift abstraction of motion through Ticket Dice, when the story accelerates.

Each layer complements the others. Caravans crawl. Daemons meddle. Journeys fast-forward. What UVG once implied—that gods and daemons tug the threads of fate—is now the explicit pulse running through every subsystem.

### Daemon Scaffolding

The Vastlands Guidebook foreshadows daemonic influence—possessions, null encounters, bloodlines. That layer crystallizes as:

* **Bonus Dice**: situational, scope-specific—travel Tickets, or others yet stranger trinkets.
* **Hero Dice**: broad daemon leverage, capped by PC level.  

## Primary & Adjacent Influences

All of these showed us that dice and resources are levers of cosmology as much as chance. They feed directly into our daemon scope.

**OSR Dungeons and Dragons (0e \- 2e)** – The Old School DNA traces back to Arneson’s Blackmoor sessions and Gygax’s Greyhawk campaigns. Their original designs were half-sandbox, half-wargame improvisation—procedures for dungeon expeditions, kingdom logistics, and emergent consequence more than plotted narrative. But these early notes and pamphlets were raw ore. It was BECMI D\&D (1983–1986), Frank Mentzer’s boxed sets, that refined them into steel. See the next section.

**JG-102 Caverns of Thracia (1979, Judges Guild, Jennell Jaquays)** – A foundational adventure, famous for “Jacquaying the dungeon.” Multi-level maps, multiple entrances, factions with agendas, and procedural density made the dungeon into a living world, not a linear gauntlet. Its design tricks—loops, dead ends with meaning, interconnected threats—are the DNA for our procedural travel events. Thracia is the OSR’s Rosetta Stone of non-linear emergent play.

**Shadowrun (1989)** – Famously crunchy, but notable for its multi-scale play: meatspace runs, matrix hacks, and astral quests overlapping. Characters are both street-level actors and pawns in megacorp/daemon-level struggles.

**Tales from the Floating Vagabond (1991)** – A parody RPG, but mechanically instructive. Rules for “Schticks” bent reality (like Toon), creating narrative physics knobs. Humor was the surface, but beneath it was a lesson: mechanics can reinforce absurdity as much as deadliness. For us, this validates daemonic meddling that ranges from slapstick to metaphysical horror.

**Earthdawn (1993)** – A pillar of our framework. Its design philosophy is tight: rules are not just mechanics, they are meta-bindings to the world’s metaphysics. Disciplines define advancement, Karma dice fuel extraordinary effort, and the cycle of Horrors creates a cosmology baked into resolution. For Daemons in the Vastlands, Earthdawn is proof that dice pools can be more than modifiers—they are a statement of cosmology. Our Bonus Dice and Hero Dice live in that lineage.

**Mage: the Ascension (1993)** – The consensus reality wars. Magic succeeds or fails based on paradigm, belief, and the collective unconscious. This is daemon play in all but name: competing metaphysical agents bending rolls, events, and outcomes according to their worldview. Mage shows how to operationalize philosophy as mechanics.

**Feng Shui (1996)** – Robin D. Laws’ action-movie RPG, built for Hong Kong cinema heroics. Its schticks and archetypes made characters feel like they’d walked off a John Woo set, while the chi war backdrop created a metaphysical struggle across time periods. Mechanically, it taught that style is system: stunts, narration, and over-the-top choices weren’t extra—they were the game. For Daemons in the Vastlands, Feng Shui reminds us that dice can be engines of spectacle, not just survival, and that hidden wars (daemons vs. gods vs. stranger things) can echo across eras.

**The Shadow of Yesterday (2004)** – A seminal indie game. Known for Keys (XP triggers based on character motives), but equally important are its Pools (resource dice for Vigor, Instinct, Reason) and its modular resolution system (simple vs. extended conflicts). Together, these made advancement a story engine and resource management a narrative driver. In our daemon frame, this maps directly onto how Hero Dice tie PC motives to daemon power: story choices → resources → cosmic leverage.

**The Mountain Witch (2005)** – Trust Dice meta economy: alliances and betrayals enforced by mechanics, not just roleplay. Direct ancestor of daemon Hero Dice.

### Classic OSR Bedrock (OSR DNA)

BECMI crystallized what OD\&D and AD\&D 1e gestured toward: a tiered scope of play.

* **Basic** taught dungeon crawling: lethal expeditions, resource management, teamwork.  
* **Expert** expanded the world: wilderness exploration, domain threats, seafaring.  
* **Companion** introduced domain rulership: characters became lords, armies mattered, wars reshaped the map.  
* **Master** pushed toward planar mastery and grand designs.  
* **Immortal** catapulted players into the divine game, shaping cosmology itself.

This was not just power inflation—it was a meta-structure of play, where characters rose from rat-bitten novices to godlike agents of reality, and the rules grew with them. Frank Mentzer’s design fingerprints carry far. The Final Fantasy series (particularly from III onward) mirrors this structure: early dungeon grinds → overworld exploration → fleet and airship travel → domain/kingdom influence → finally confronting cosmic/metaphysical foes. Many JRPGs are effectively BECMI campaigns in digital form.

Later editions of D\&D (2e metaplot-heavy era, 3e tactical focus, 4e encounter grids, 5e narrative heroic bias) abandoned these tiered scopes. The link between dungeon expeditions, wilderness travel, domain rulership, and cosmic stakes was broken into isolated playstyles. The OSR revival seizes on this loss, insisting that the old scope-shifting chassis is what made the original designs powerful. Significant callouts for good 2e design include Planescape and Spelljammer.

Thus, our daemon framework inherits not just mechanics from B/X and BECMI, but the philosophy of tiered escalation:

* PCs slog through caravans and voyage events.  
* Daemons rise alongside them, spending Hero Dice to twist fate.  
* Eventually, PCs and Daemons collide with gods and stranger things—the Immortal tier reborn, where player roles themselves stretch beyond the human scale.

### Modern OSR Revival

When the OSR movement caught fire in the 2000s, it wasn’t simply nostalgia. It was an act of archaeology—digging through the strata of OD\&D, B/X, and BECMI to unearth what had been lost when later editions trimmed away scope, lethality, and procedural depth. Out of that excavation grew a wave of games that reinterpreted those bones through modern aesthetics, layout, and philosophy.

**Dungeon Crawl Classics (2003, reissued 2012\)** – Took OD\&D’s deadliness and turned the dial to eleven. Famous for its funnel (players run a horde of peasants; survivors become PCs) and weird dice chains. Its core ethos: magic should be dangerous, play should be unpredictable.

**OSRIC (2006)** – A restatement of AD\&D 1e. Dense, table-heavy, but vital: it kept the Gygaxian chassis alive for grognards and new players alike.

**Labyrinth Lord (2007)** – A faithful B/X retroclone, often credited as the spark of the OSR publishing boom. It proved you could publish “out of print” rules legally by restating them, and it set the tone for modular hackability.

**Lamentations of the Flame Princess (2009)** – B/X mechanics fused with early modern horror and surreal weirdness. Its modules (e.g. Death Frost Doom, Better Than Any Man) are beloved for their ability to shock, punish, and inspire emergent chaos.

**Into the Odd (2014) / Electric Bastionland (2019)** – Radical minimalism. No to-hit rolls, auto-damage, strange industrial ruins. Character backgrounds read like microfiction. Play emerges from brutal clarity.

**Troika\! (2019)** – A surreal OSR cousin built on Fighting Fantasy rules. Character creation by rolling on backgrounds that drip with tone (“Crotchet Mystic,” “Zoanthrop”). Its procedural encounter tables and gonzo tone mirror UVG’s psychedelic caravans.

**Mörk Borg (2019)** – Doom-metal aesthetic manifesto masquerading as an RPG. Lean rules, heavy art, apocalyptic countdown clocks. A lesson that OSR is as much about attitude and aesthetic violence as mechanics.

**Old School Essentials (2019)** – A polished, modern restatement of B/X. Clear rules, modular expansions. Considered by many the definitive OSR core.

**Knave (2018), Cairn (2020)** – Toolkit RPGs designed to be ultra-hackable. Strip away fluff, keep procedures. They embody the OSR’s “design for play, not lore” mindset.

This revival reframed the conversation: the value wasn’t just in lethality or random tables, but in scope and modularity. Games could be light but still procedural; weird but still lethal; aesthetic but still emergent.

For Daemons in the Vastlands, this modern OSR wave is proof that we can take the BECMI tiered structure and Earthdawn’s metaphysical bindings, then lace them with modern weirdness. Hero Dice can be as strange as a Mörk Borg relic; Bonus Dice can echo The Mountain Witch’s trust pool. And the whole aesthetic can be dressed in UVG’s psychedelic wasteland, with daemons whispering behind every die roll.

### **Why This Matters for *Elyncia***

*FTLS* is not merely a new entry in the OSR or a strange cousin to *Ultraviolet Grasslands*—it is a philosophical continuation of a half-century of design experiments about how chance, myth, and agency intertwine. Every die rolled at the table is a metaphysical statement. Every subsystem—caravan, daemon, or dream—arises from that long conversation between probability and meaning.

From **Earthdawn**, we inherit the creed that mechanics *are* metaphysics. There, dice measure not skill but spiritual resonance—the way a character’s discipline aligns with the world’s hidden harmonics. Likewise, our **Hero Dice** are not luck points to be hoarded or burned for convenience. They are the daemon’s breath, the visible condensation of unseen intent. When a player spends one, they are declaring that destiny itself bends—just slightly—in their daemon’s favor.

From **Caverns of Thracia**, we inherit structure as revelation. The dungeon, with its loops, factions, and secret stairs, is not only a map—it’s an epistemology. Knowledge comes from movement, discovery, and loss. Translating that into the steppe and astral seas, our *voyage events* and *daemon interventions* become procedural doorways. Each roll opens or closes routes across fate’s terrain. The adventure is not plotted; it *emerges*, just as Thracia’s stories do, from the patient conversation between dice and decision.

From **BECMI’s tiered play**, we inherit the ladder of transcendence. Those boxed sets did not merely increase power—they expanded *scope*. Basic was the crawl. Expert was the journey. Companion was command. Master was myth. Immortal was cosmology itself. In our design, that escalator returns. PCs begin as caravaners trudging through dust; their daemons meddle from beyond. In time, those daemons may become gods, and the gods may, in turn, become rules. The table’s dice become the pantheon’s language.

From the **classic OSR** corpus we inherit the severe, procedural truths:  
 maps and story over numbers, choice over balance, consequence over comfort.  
 Pre-roll. Record. Replace. Let the world respond, not conspire.  
 Death is not a failure state—it is punctuation.

From the **modern OSR revival**, we inherit audacity. The courage to treat the rulebook as collage, to splice neon surrealism onto rusted mechanics. Games like *Mörk Borg* and *Troika\!* proved that form and color can be philosophy; weirdness can be rigor. So, our daemons may wear bone masks or ultraviolet halos. They may whisper in algebra or scream in vaporwave fonts. But the system underneath still hums with procedural integrity.

Together these lineages converge into one conviction:  
 the **Daemon layer is the Immortal tier reborn.**  
 It restores the lost dialogue between the mortal and the cosmic, between dice and destiny. What Mentzer built as ascension through levels, what Earthdawn encoded as karmic recursion, we reinterpret as dual-scale play: the player as both pawn and playwright.

When a group sits down to play, they are not only crossing deserts or seas—they are participating in an experiment about agency itself. The rules are cosmology. The dice are philosophy. And the act of play is the daemon’s dream, folding the mortal crawl into the metaphysical game above.
