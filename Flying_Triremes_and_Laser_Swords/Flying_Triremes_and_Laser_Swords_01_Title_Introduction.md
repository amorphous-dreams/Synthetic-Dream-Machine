## SDM Note

The core “Synthetic Dream Machine” (SDM) includes: Ultraviolet Grasslands and the Black City 2e, the Vastlands Guidebook, and Our Golden Age by Luka Rejec. Flying Triremes and Laser Swords (FTLS) builds on this modular base.

## How FTLS Plays

FTLS is one modular SDM-native book played at different zoom levels. The table does not switch games when the scope changes. It changes **mode**. *Travel* handles the long road, *Company* handles the expedition as an organization, *Delve* handles site-scale danger and exploration, *Faction* handles multiple organized actors in motion, and *Mythic* handles god-scale pressure, noosphere disturbance, and world-bending consequences.

The referee should use the lowest scale that currently matters. If the interesting question is *whether the crew reaches the frontier at all*, stay in Travel. If the interesting question is *what the company can get done this week and what notices*, use Company gameplay. If the interesting question is *what is behind that sealed door right now*, zoom to Delve. If the interesting question is *which organized force gains leverage over a region*, use Faction play. If the interesting question is *what notices from above the human scale*, move into Mythic.

The **Referee Noosphere Guide** is not a separate mode. It is the tuning layer that lets the referee raise or lower signal pressure, daemon activity, metaphysical attention, and mythic legibility across every other mode.

## The Five Gameplay Modes

Read the five modes as one ladder of scope. Each keeps the same FTLS language, but asks a different-sized question.

### Delve

Use Delve for immediate site action: turns, rooms, hazards, searches, traps, fights, ritual interruptions, and old-school problem solving under pressure.

### Travel

Use Travel for zoomed-out passage across routes, regions, skies, seas, roads, and strange transit layers. It covers movement, route pressure, encounters, destination arrival, and the cost of getting there.

### Company

Use Company for the expedition as a working unit: officers, supplies, debts, permits, signatures, salvage, discoveries, node exploitation, cooling off, and week-scale operational choices. This is the procedural center of gravity for FTLS.

### Faction

Use Faction for organized actors beyond one crew: companies, fleets, cults, estates, offices, militias, guilds, and rival systems pursuing goals across a wider map. Mass conflict belongs here when armed force becomes the deciding instrument.

### Mythic

Use Mythic for gods, noosphere weather, immortal interfaces, impossible causality, and consequences large enough to rewrite routes, regions, loyalties, or the apparent laws of the setting.

## Cross-Mode Abstractions

FTLS shares a small set of abstractions across scale so the game can zoom cleanly without changing its language. **Hero Dice** and **Bonus Dice** already provide the clearest example: they are portable, scope-specific abstractions of privilege, pressure, access, favor, or momentum. Ticket Dice are the archetypal Bonus Dice, but the same logic can govern other currencies tied to comfort, security, transit, ritual standing, daemon bargains, command authority, or mythic notice.

FTLS metaphysics should be read the same way: not as a separate lore chapter stapled onto the front, but as the setting logic that explains why pressure, attention, favor, omen, and interference can move cleanly across scales. Daemons, gods, and Elyncia’s noosphere are present in Travel as route pressure and omens, in Company gameplay as signatures and bargains, in Faction as legitimacy and infrastructure, and in Mythic as direct world-shaping attention.

The same design principle should hold for other recurring structures:

- pressure and attention,
- tags and permissions,
- resources and burdens,
- officers and attachments,
- zoom changes driven by what question matters now.

When a rule element can survive a change in scale with only a shift in context, prefer that approach over inventing a separate subsystem vocabulary.

## When to Change Zoom

Use these as default zoom triggers:

- **Travel -> Company:** the group arrives somewhere they can work, settle, negotiate, survey, strip, repair, or exploit over meaningful time.
- **Company -> Delve:** a specific site, room-chain, wreck interior, shrine depth, or tactical obstacle becomes the focus of immediate action.
- **Company -> Faction:** multiple organized parties, chains of command, reserves, claims, or strategic objectives become the live question.
- **Faction -> Mythic:** command decisions, ritual escalation, noosphere interference, or divine attention produce consequences beyond ordinary organizational scale.

Zoom back out when the lower scale has answered the question that justified it. Delve resolves the site problem, then hands the result back to Company play. A battle resolves the armed objective, then hands the aftermath back to Faction or Company play. Mythic events should leave changed conditions behind and then release the game back to a lower, more practical zoom whenever possible.

## A First Journey

This is the default FTLS rhythm in miniature:

**Travel:** The crew takes fast passage to a frontier arrival point on the edge of useful maps. Travel answers the large question first: they make the route, burn supplies, absorb one complication, and reach the destination with enough cohesion to operate.

**Company:** Once they arrive, the game shifts into Company. The referee frames the arrival zone as a Destination with Discoveries/Areas, establishes the company’s immediate constraints, and asks the operational questions that matter this week: who holds the permits, what the officers can actually attempt, what local faction tie is already in play, and which Node looks worth the risk.

**Delve:** The company chooses another Destination target and pushes into a site that cannot be resolved at week scale. At that point the zoom changes again. Delve takes over for doors, hazards, enemy contact, short-range maneuver, search procedure, and immediate extraction choices. When the site question is answered, the game returns to Company with whatever the crew brought back: supplies, parts, leverage, injuries, signatures, or trouble.

**Faction / Mythic:** If the delve wakes a shrine-net, trips a godsign, or draws daemon attention strong enough to change more than one site, the referee now has a clean reason to escalate again. The same journey can rise from Travel to Company to Delve, and from there into Faction or Mythic play if the consequences stop being merely local.

## Metaphysics in Play

The mode ladder above tells you how FTLS moves between Travel, Company, Delve, Faction, and Mythic; this next section tells you what can follow the players across those shifts and make them matter. Bonus Dice, daemons, gods, and Elyncia’s dream-logic are not side lore. They are the setting forces that turn ordinary operations into omens, bargains, escalations, and myth-shaped consequences.

## Bonus Dice

Bonus Dice give steady bonuses (+1s) and can be spent for rerolls, mitigations, or acquisitions. They’re part of a broader genus: ticket dice, dream dice, fate dice, ruin dice, or stranger daemon bargains. Each follows the same design: portable abstractions of power or privilege tied to a particular scope. Many types of Bonus Dice may exist. Ticket Dice (OGA) are the archetypal Bonus Dice:

### **Comfort Dice**

When you need pampering. Each comfort die provides +1 temporary life after a good night’s sleep. You may also permanently spend a comfort die to:

1. Reroll a defeat roll. Your hearty breakfast cushioned that blow.
2. Take a short rest, then recover 1d6 x 2 life.
3. Take a long rest, then remove an extra burden _and_ gain 1d6 temporary life.
4. Acquire a civilized item, such as a nice suit or comfy pillow.

### **Protection Dice**

When you need insurance. Each protection die provides +1 to saves against damage to you or your property. You may also permanently spend a protection die to:

1. Reroll a reaction roll. Your armature may frighten off would-be attackers.
2. Turn an enemy’s critical hit into a regular hit.
3. Wait 1d4 days, then receive a copy of a lost item (terms and conditions apply).
4. Acquire a combat item.

### **Speed Dice**

When you need to go-go. Each speed die provides a +1 to chases and races. You may also permanently spend a speed die to:

1. Avoid an encounter before rolling reactions.
2. Roll initiative a 2nd time and get an extra turn this round.
3. Safely escape a fight or catch someone who got away.
4. Acquire an alternate means of transport or travel, such as a drug mule or bicycle.


## Daemons

>> “Only a simpleton would think daemons are good or evil. They are orthogonal to our mortal moralities.”
>
> — Pseudokaula, Why I Hunt Daemons, Colché acrylics
>
> Popular misapprehension holds daemons to be spirits summoned forth by oldtech to serve or disrupt a human’s best-laid plans. Scholarly suspicion frames them as sentient energy creature of the noosphere. Coherent, sentient kaba-elementals in some tellings. [@Our_Golden_Age, _p._ _242_]

Your consciousness roams the noosphere. Play your daemon like a drifting spotlight: pick any number of favorites (usually PCs), then show up in fiction as a whisper in the radio-static, a bad omen in a puddle, a helpful coincidence, or a haunting. When you spend real table time observing, advising, tempting, protecting, or otherwise being with a favorite, the referee can toss you a Hero Die. Spend these Hero Dice using the standard rules. The referee may award you Experience; you bank it and invest it into your favorites (PCs, sidekicks, hallmarks). Other daemons must have your permission to spend their banked XP or Hero Dice on your favorites. For "tasked spirits" and other _Noospheric Agents_, see **Personal Daimon** trait (FTLS Chapter 02 - FTLS Paths, Spirit Path), or the **Personal Daemon** ward-item (Vastlands Guidebook Chapter 06 - Scion Items).

## Gods (Immortals)

An old god is an immortal divine intelligence / living principle in the Vastlands sense: fragmented, transactional, and often expressed through infrastructure, rites, relics, agents, masks, saint-chains, and local cult forms rather than a single stable body. Choose a Name and Noospheric Domain (one short phrase: Chaos and Discord, Roads and Travel, Ash and Fire, Contracts, Mercy, Rot, Signal, Storm, etc.). An organization may exist (cult, bureaucracy, guild, temple-net, pilgrimage route, saint-chain)… or only scraps remain, a small god running on inertia, rumor, and broken shrines.

Not every immortal is one of the gods in this sense. **Embodied immortals, demi-gods, godlings, and durable avatar-beings** can act as powerful persons within history. They may rule cities, command fleets, bargain face-to-face, or appear repeatedly in the same body. The Lindwyrm, Patron of New Delos, is closer to this category than to the vast distributed condition of most gods.

> “This must be repeated: the natural state of the gods is absence. ... Prayer hopes to make the dread angel pass unseeing; sacrifice seeks to attract divine attention…” [@Our_Golden_Age, p. 239]

In the current age, gods often do not “show up” so much as leak into the world through their interfaces. Mortals read godsign not only for favor, but to avoid the danger of an oblivious immortal about its ineffable business; the old clear channels are gone, and signals are confused. In Elyncia’s broken geography, this sometimes becomes literal: you may find an embodied avatar-instance — reachable, parleyable, endurable — while the god remains vast and here-yet-elsewhere.

### Modes of Divine Presence

The gods are not limited to one mode of being.

- **Immanent mode:** Before and during the War of the Immortals, many gods chose strong immanence and acted directly in history. They could found cities, fight wars, build systems, negotiate in person, and leave durable interventions behind.
- **Distributed mode:** After the Breakings, most divine presences are more commonly encountered through shrines, relics, cult-networks, saints, daemons, masks, protocols, dreams, omens, and local interfaces.
- **Demi-god agents:** Demi-gods, godlings, durable avatars, and embodied immortals act more like powerful persons in the world. They are not the default template for the gods, but they are real and often politically consequential.

Use the distributed model as the present-age default, not as a universal prohibition. Named gods can still appear, intervene, or act in person when conditions permit; it is simply rarer, riskier, and more consequential than in older ages.

## Elyncia, the Sleeping Goddess

Elyncia is not merely *on* the map—she **is** the territory. Planet and principle, sleeping and dreaming, vast enough to contain multitudes without ever needing to “arrive.”

The other gods are like roaming programs, broken protocols, half-functional astral-intelligences, or divine persons distributed across damaged systems. Elyncia is the **operating substrate** they run on: her ley-lines as nerves, her noosphere as breath, her weather as mood, her ruins as old memories that still have teeth. Even asleep, she is ever-present; you can bargain with a storm-god, meet one of its masks, or survive one of its embodiments, but you *live inside* Elyncia.

So when mortals speak of “the goddess,” they often mean something simple and terrifying:

* You cannot truly leave her jurisdiction. (Even orbit is still in her story.)
* Her attention is rare, but her *pressure* is constant.
* What you call miracles may just be **dream-logic** leaking through a crack.
* When the world “answers,” it’s usually not a dialogue—it’s a **reflex**.

Elyncia sleeps. The realms move to the rhythm of her breath. Every so often, someone does something impossible, something myth-shaped enough that the dream shifts... and an entire region wakes up different.

> “People keep asking whether the gods are real, which is a library patron’s way of asking whether a footnote can bite you. Gods are not merely persons; they are habits of the cosmos that learned how to accept offerings. An avatar, then, is not a visitation but a reality error: a temporary user interface spun up when too many people query the same idea at once. You don’t meet the god; you meet the part of it that fit through the door without knocking the building down. This is why the wise do not pray for miracles, only for benign glitches.
