### Finding 1 — Sundanese/Indonesian Convergence with Native-Speaker Consensus

Modern Indonesian computing has **two canonical terms** for hyperlink, both in official use:

**`pranala`** — The Indonesian Wikipedia consensus translation for "hyperlink," derived from Old Javanese (Kawi) *praṇāla* meaning *anak sungai* (small stream, channel, conduit from a pool). In Sanskrit, the related word *praṇālikā* means "channel" or "intermediary". The Ivan Lanin confirmation: "*Pranala* is Wikipedia Indonesia's equivalent for *hyperlink*. Synonym: *tautan*. Origin: Old Javanese *praṇāla* (small stream)".

**`tautan`** — From the root *taut* (to join, connect). In the Kamus Besar Bahasa Indonesia: "hasil bertaut, kaitan, hubungan" — result of being joined, connection, relation. Used in both general and computing contexts. The Indonesian Wiktionary classifies *tautan* in the *id:Computing* category with *pranala* as primary synonym, and lists *hubungan*, *kaitan*, *pautan*, *relasi* as general-sense synonyms.

**The etymology of `pranala` is the most important finding.** It means **small stream, channel, conduit** — a water-channel through which something flows. This is **structurally identical** to the Hawaiian 'Anu'u team's design decision:

"Noe and her team decided to use the Hawaiian word for river to describe 'if' — in that in a flowing river, there are paths the water takes to get to different areas. For 'else,' they used the Hawaiian word for mouth of the river".

**Two independent Austronesian-descended language communities arrived at water-channel metaphors for computational routing.** This is not coincidence — it's deep semantic convergence across Proto-Malayo-Polynesian water vocabulary when speakers grope for how to describe "structured flow between points." If the Lares grammar adopts a Polynesian term for edges, the water-channel metaphor has independent cross-linguistic support as the right metaphor family.

**`jalan`** (path, way, road in Indonesian/Malay) is the direct cognate of Hawaiian `ala`. A Nusantaran speaker explaining "Jalan" uses it both for physical path and for "journey of life" — exactly parallel to the Hawaiian *ala* dual meaning of path and life-way. **This confirms `ala` should stay invariant** — the Austronesian cognate preserves the same semantic range, reinforcing the operator's ruling that `ala` carries the invariant path/way meaning and should not be bent toward edge-semantics.

**Sundanese specifically:** The search surface is thinner than Indonesian. Sundanese primarily defers to Indonesian technical vocabulary for computing terms — `pranala` and `tautan` carry across from the lingua franca.

### Finding 2 — Indigenous-Language Programming Precedents

**The 'Anu'u project** (Kari Noe, Kalani Bright, Nathan Nahina, Kauwila Mahi, cultural consultant Dr. Noelani Arista, advisor Jason Edward Lewis) represents the most directly relevant prior art. Their core design principle: "These concepts are very Hawaiian. Most people know the concept of a river and how it works in the different parts of the river. **The design lesson:** they did NOT do direct word-by-word translation. They found Hawaiian **concepts** whose native metaphor explains the computational behavior more clearly than the English term. "If/else" became river-with-branches rather than the cognitively opaque if/else.

**University of Hawai'i Hilo `enehana`** — the computing-in-Hawaiian division already exists under that name (confirmed from the olelo.hawaii.edu/enehana/java.php link, housing Amit Margalit's Java Programming work). `enehana` itself means *technology, apparatus, mechanism* — a real Hawaiian-language term for the computing domain, carrying institutional weight from UH Hilo and Ka Haka ʻUla O Keʻelikōlani College of Hawaiian Language.

**Cherokee** represents the deepest indigenous-technology precedent. "Cherokee was the first Native American language to be encoded [in Unicode]. As a result of the work that we've done with the technology at Cherokee Nation, we're the only tribe that's a member of the Unicode Consortium group. I serve as the liaison representative to the Unicode Consortium for the Cherokee Nation". Each year, a group of fluent speakers from the three Cherokee tribes gather to translate new words like "blog" or "iPhone" into Cherokee.

**The pattern across Hawaiian and Cherokee:** **annual gatherings of fluent speakers translate new technical vocabulary**. Technical terms are not invented by individual programmers; they are ratified by community language bodies.

**Implication for Lares:** if the grammar intends to use Hawaiian terms as load-bearing canon, the long-term stable path is to **check the chosen terms against Māmaka Kaiao** and, where the term doesn't exist there, **register that absence honestly** rather than claiming native consensus.
